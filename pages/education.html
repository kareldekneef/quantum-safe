The Quantum Threat Explained

Why Current Encryption Will Fail

Today's public-key encryption (RSA, ECC) relies on mathematical problems that classical computers find extremely difficult to solve:

  • RSA: Based on the difficulty of factoring large numbers (e.g., finding the two prime factors of a 2048-bit number)
  • ECC: Based on the discrete logarithm problem on elliptic curves

A classical supercomputer would need longer than the age of the universe to break RSA-2048. But in 1994, mathematician Peter Shor developed an algorithm that, running on a sufficiently powerful quantum computer, could break RSA-2048 in hours.

Harvest Now, Decrypt Later (HNDL)

Here's the uncomfortable reality: adversaries don't need to wait for quantum computers to benefit from them.

The Attack Pattern:

  1. Today: Intercept and store encrypted data (network traffic, stored files, communications)
  2. Store: Keep the encrypted data in archives
  3. Later: When quantum computers mature, decrypt the historical data

Nation-state actors are already doing this. If your data will still be sensitive in 10-15 years, it's at risk today.

The Timeline: What the Experts Say

The Global Risk Institute's 2024 Quantum Threat Timeline Report (47 expert survey, December 2024) puts it in concrete terms:

5–14% Probability of a cryptographically relevant quantum computer within 5 years
19–34% Probability within 10 years — and rising year-over-year
~79% Probability by 2044 — effectively inevitable on a 20-year horizon

NSA, CISA, NCSC, BSI, and ENISA all share the same working assumption: a CRQC is not imminent but is plausible within the decade. Given that enterprise migrations take 5–10 years, the rational response is to start now.

Understanding Quantum Computing

Classical Bits vs Qubits

Classical bit: Either 0 or 1. Like a light switch - on or off.

Qubit: Can be 0, 1, or a "superposition" of both simultaneously. Like a coin spinning in the air - it's both heads and tails until it lands.

This superposition allows quantum computers to explore many possibilities at once, making them powerful for specific problems - including breaking encryption.

Why "1 Million Qubits" Headlines Are Misleading

There's a critical distinction between physical qubits and logical qubits:

Physical Qubits

  • Actual hardware components
  • ~1% error rate (very high)
  • Lose quantum properties quickly
  • Current systems: 50-1,000

Logical Qubits

  • Created from many physical qubits
  • Error-corrected and reliable
  • What's needed for real computation
  • ~500 physical = 1 logical

Breaking RSA-2048 requires approximately 4,000 logical qubits. With current technology, that means roughly 2 million physical qubits with error correction - far beyond today's capabilities.

Decoherence: Why Quantum Computers Are Hard to Build

Qubits are extremely fragile. Any interaction with the environment - heat, vibration, electromagnetic radiation - causes "decoherence," destroying the quantum state.

That's why quantum computers operate at temperatures colder than outer space (-273°C) and require extraordinary isolation. Building reliable, large-scale quantum computers is one of the greatest engineering challenges of our time.

Post-Quantum Cryptography

A New Mathematical Foundation

Post-quantum cryptography uses mathematical problems that remain hard for both classical and quantum computers. Unlike RSA's factoring problem, these don't have efficient quantum solutions.

The Five NIST-Approved Algorithms

After an 8-year process, NIST has standardised or selected five post-quantum algorithms across two mathematical families — a deliberate hedge against future cryptanalytic breakthroughs.

FIPS 203 — Final

ML-KEM

Module-Lattice Key Encapsulation Mechanism (formerly CRYSTALS-Kyber)

Purpose: Key exchange and data encryption

Use Cases: TLS connections, encrypted storage, secure communications

Based On: Lattice mathematics (Module-LWE)

Why choose it: Fastest performance, smallest key sizes — primary choice for most deployments

FIPS 204 — Final

ML-DSA

Module-Lattice Digital Signature Algorithm (formerly CRYSTALS-Dilithium)

Purpose: Digital signatures

Use Cases: Code signing, document authentication, certificate chains

Based On: Lattice mathematics

Why choose it: Primary signature algorithm — best balance of speed and key size

FIPS 205 — Final

SLH-DSA

Stateless Hash-Based Digital Signature (formerly SPHINCS+)

Purpose: Conservative digital signatures

Use Cases: Long-term signature verification, firmware signing, high-assurance contexts

Based On: Hash functions — entirely different mathematics from ML-DSA

Why choose it: Most conservative option; use when you want a fallback that doesn't rely on lattice assumptions

FIPS 206 — Draft 2025

FN-DSA

Falcon-based Digital Signature Algorithm (formerly FALCON)

Purpose: Digital signatures with compact keys

Use Cases: Constrained environments, IoT, embedded systems requiring small signatures

Based On: NTRU lattices — different from ML-DSA's lattice approach

Why choose it: Smallest signature size of the lattice-based options; FIPS 206 draft submitted August 2025, final expected 2026–2027

Selected March 2025

HQC

Hamming Quasi-Cyclic

Purpose: Key encapsulation — backup to ML-KEM

Use Cases: High-assurance environments that require algorithm diversity

Based On: Error-correcting codes — entirely different mathematics from lattice-based algorithms

Why choose it: Selected specifically as a hedge: if a breakthrough breaks lattice mathematics, HQC remains secure. Draft standard expected 2026.

Why Algorithm Diversity Is a Strategic Decision

NIST chose five algorithms — not one — deliberately. Three rely on lattice mathematics; two use entirely different foundations (hash functions, error-correcting codes). This is a hedge: if future research finds an efficient quantum attack on lattice problems, the hash-based and code-based algorithms remain secure.

What this means for your organisation: Deploying only ML-KEM + ML-DSA is a reasonable starting point — but high-assurance environments (financial infrastructure, long-lived classified data, critical national infrastructure) should plan for algorithm diversity from the outset. Crypto agility makes this possible without a full rearchitecture later.

Lattice-based cryptography has been studied for over 30 years. While no formal proof of hardness exists, extensive cryptanalysis has found no efficient quantum attacks — and the same is true of RSA, which has no formal proof either.

Implementation Strategies

Hybrid Cryptography: The Recommended Approach

Use both classical and post-quantum algorithms simultaneously. If either proves vulnerable, the other still protects you.

Hybrid TLS Handshake
RSA/ECC Classical Key
+
ML-KEM PQC Key
=
Combined Session Key

Hybrid PQC is already the internet standard. By March 2025, 38% of all HTTPS traffic on Cloudflare ran over hybrid PQC. Chrome and Firefox ship X25519+ML-KEM on by default as of August 2025. Safari joins with iOS 26 / macOS Tahoe. Your vendors are not waiting for you.

Crypto Agility: Future-Proofing Your Systems

Design systems so cryptographic algorithms can be changed without major overhauls:

  • Abstraction: Don't hardcode algorithms in application logic
  • Configuration: Make algorithm selection configurable
  • Standard Libraries: Use maintained crypto libraries (OpenSSL, BoringSSL)
  • Key Management: Ensure KMS/HSM can support larger PQC keys

Hardware Milestones: Why the Threat Is Accelerating

Two 2024–2025 breakthroughs changed the expert consensus on how quickly fault-tolerant quantum computers could arrive:

December 2024

Google Willow

105-qubit superconducting processor achieved exponential error suppression — error rates fell as more qubits were added, the opposite of what was seen before. This crossed a key threshold toward fault-tolerant quantum computing.

February 2025

Microsoft Majorana 1

First topological qubit processor, based on an entirely different physical approach designed to be inherently more stable. Microsoft's stated goal: scale to 1 million qubits on a single chip.

2029 target

IBM Quantum Starling

IBM's roadmap targets 200 error-corrected logical qubits capable of running ~100 million operations. This would be the first system capable of meaningful fault-tolerant computation at scale.

None of these systems can break RSA-2048 today. But each one reduces the engineering gap that remains. Recent 2025 research has also significantly reduced the estimated qubit requirements for breaking RSA-2048 — some models now suggest fewer than 1 million physical qubits under optimised architectures, down from earlier estimates of 20+ million.

PQC migration is no longer just a security best practice — it is becoming a compliance obligation. The following deadlines are binding for their respective jurisdictions and sectors.

What This Means for Financial Services

Financial institutions face the most immediate regulatory pressure:

  • EU DORA (in force now): The Digital Operational Resilience Act explicitly requires financial entities to address quantum risk as part of ICT risk management. Supervisory authorities are beginning to ask about PQC roadmaps in their 2025 review cycles.
  • EU NIS CG 2030 target: High-risk use cases — including critical financial systems — must be transitioned by 2030. That leaves roughly 5 years, which is within the migration timeline for a complex financial institution.
  • Basel / BIS: The Committee on Payments and Market Infrastructures has flagged quantum risk in operational resilience guidance. While no standalone PQC regulation exists at the BIS level yet, expect this to change as 2030 approaches.

The 2025 urgency point: A financial institution with a 7-year data sensitivity period that begins its migration in 2026 and takes 4 years will finish in 2030. Under Mosca's Theorem, that is borderline. Any delay compounds the risk — and may also put the institution in breach of DORA's resilience requirements.
